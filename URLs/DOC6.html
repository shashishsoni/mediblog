Shortness of Breath? Not Asthma? May Be the Cause of Heart Problems

Generalised convulsive status epilepticus is a medical emergency characterized by a generalized convulsion lasting 30 minutes or longer, or when successive convulsions occur so frequently over a 30-minute period that the patient does not recover consciousness between them. The exact onset and duration of status epilepticus are often difficult to ascertain, but it is crucial to initiate seizure management promptly due to the potential for neuronal damage and poor neurological outcomes. In the community setting, first-line treatment involves general protective measures to ensure the safety and well-being of the individual experiencing status epilepticus. These measures include placing the person in a recovery position, ensuring protection of the head, releasing any tight clothing, and moving away from dangerous positions. It is important not to put anything in the person's mouth and to keep their head slightly lower if vomiting occurs. Maintaining composure and keeping track of time are essential during this critical situation. If the seizure continues for 5 minutes or more, it is recommended to administer intranasal midazolam as prescribed by a healthcare professional. Intranasal midazolam has been shown to effectively terminate seizures, with up to 90% of convulsions stopping within 5 minutes without any treatment. Additionally, if intravenous access is available in a hospital setting with resuscitation facilities, intravenous lorazepam can be administered as a first-line treatment. In the hospital setting, the priority is to ensure the ABCs (Airway, Breathing, and Circulation) of the individual before initiating any pharmacologic intervention. Placing the patient in the lateral decubitus position helps prevent aspiration of emesis and closure of the epiglottis over the glottis. Monitoring the person's airway, breathing, circulation, and other vital signs is crucial, especially if it is the first episode requiring emergency treatment or if there are concerns or difficulties in monitoring these parameters. Depending on the response to treatment and the individual's specific situation, a personalized care plan should be implemented. If the seizure continues for over five minutes after emergency medication administration, or if there are concerns about the individual's airway, breathing, circulation, or other vital signs, it is advisable to call for an ambulance for further evaluation and management in a hospital setting.
