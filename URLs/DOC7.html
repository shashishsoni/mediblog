Shortness of Breath? Not Asthma? May Be the Cause of Heart Problems

Generalised convulsive status epilepticus is a severe medical condition characterized by a generalized convulsion lasting 30 minutes or longer, or when successive convulsions occur so frequently over a 30-minute period that the patient does not regain consciousness between them. However, determining the exact onset and duration of status epilepticus can be challenging. Recognizing that a seizure lasting for 5 minutes or more may result in neuronal damage and poor neurological outcomes, it is standard practice to initiate seizure management if the duration exceeds 5 minutes. In the community setting, the first-line treatment for generalised convulsive status epilepticus involves implementing general protective measures to ensure the safety and well-being of the individual experiencing the seizure. These measures include placing the person in a recovery position, protecting the head, releasing any tight clothing, and moving away from potentially dangerous positions. It is essential to refrain from placing anything in the person's mouth and to maintain a slightly lower head position if vomiting occurs. Keeping calm and monitoring the duration of the seizure are crucial during this critical period. If the seizure persists for 5 minutes or longer, intranasal midazolam can be administered as first-line treatment as prescribed by a healthcare professional. Intranasal midazolam has demonstrated effectiveness in terminating seizures, with up to 90% of convulsions ceasing within 5 minutes without additional treatment. In cases where intravenous access is available in a hospital setting with resuscitation facilities, intravenous lorazepam can be administered. Response to treatment, the individual's situation, and any personalized care plans should guide further management decisions. In particular, if the seizure continues for more than five minutes after emergency medication administration or if there are concerns about the person's airway, breathing, circulation, or other vital signs, it is advisable to seek urgent medical attention by calling an ambulance. In a hospital setting, the priority is to ensure the ABCs (Airway, Breathing, and Circulation) of the individual before initiating any pharmacologic intervention. Placing the patient in the lateral decubitus position helps prevent aspiration of emesis and closure of the epiglottis over the glottis. Monitoring the person's airway, breathing, circulation, and vital signs is essential, especially during the first episode requiring emergency treatment or in cases where monitoring difficulties arise.
